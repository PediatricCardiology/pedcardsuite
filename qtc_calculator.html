Think of QTc like adjusting a photo's brightness for different lighting conditions. The raw QT interval changes with heart rate — faster heart = shorter QT. QTc is the "corrected" QT, normalized to a heart rate of 60 bpm, so you can compare apples to apples.

Formulas Used
Bazett
QTc = QT ÷ √(RR_sec)
Most widely used. Overcorrects at fast rates, undercorrects at slow rates.
Fridericia
QTc = QT ÷ ∛(RR_sec)
Preferred in drug trials. More accurate at extremes of HR.
Hodges
QTc = QT + 1.75 × (HR − 60)
Linear formula. Good for clinical bedside use.
Framingham
QTc = QT + 154 × (1 − RR_sec)
(Sagie 1992) Accurate across wide HR range. Common in research.
⚠ QT should be measured from onset of QRS to end of T-wave in the same beat. Use 3–5 consecutive beats and average. Leads II, V5 recommended. Avoid U-wave inclusion.
Normal QTc ranges vary by age and sex. After puberty, females have longer QTc than males — accounting for their higher risk of drug-induced TdP. Values below use Bazett formula.

Clinical Pearls
▸ Neonates (day 3–4): QTc normally higher (~400ms), use age-specific norms
▸ Prolongation in neonate can indicate LQT1/2/3 — screen parents too
▸ Drug-induced QTp: QTc >500ms or ↑ >60ms from baseline = stop drug
▸ QTc >600ms → extreme risk, consider ICU monitoring
▸ Bazett overcorrects at HR >100 — use Fridericia/Hodges in tachycardia
Conditions Causing QTc Prolongation
Genetic / Channelopathies
▸ LQTS type 1, 2, 3 (most common)
▸ Jervell & Lange-Nielsen syndrome
▸ Timothy syndrome (LQT8)
▸ Andersen-Tawil (LQT7)
Acquired / Metabolic
▸ Hypokalemia, Hypomagnesemia
▸ Hypocalcemia
▸ Hypothyroidism
▸ Myocarditis, CHD post-op
Drugs (common in pediatrics)
▸ Antibiotics: azithromycin, clarithromycin
▸ Antifungals: fluconazole
▸ Antiemetics: ondansetron, metoclopramide
▸ Antipsychotics: haloperidol, risperidone
Cardiac
▸ Complete heart block
▸ Post-cardiac arrest (CNS anoxia)
▸ Intracranial hypertension
▸ Post-op (Jatene, Fontan, etc.)
